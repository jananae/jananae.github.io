Skynet Playing Cards

A set of playing cards designed after a friend group

Overview

"Skynet" is the nickname affectionately given to a group of friends I made through high school robotics. All of us shared a mutual friend/mentor, Clayton, who was a college student when the majority of us were in high school. Every so often one of us who lived near his college would spot him, and inform the rest of us (and him) that he'd been seen. We would do the same whenever we saw him at robotics events, or when he came to help us out in our media or PR activities. With how often we saw him, Clayton jokingly compared us to the fictional Skynet from the Terminator series, and the name stuck.

About a year later, a different friend of mine had the idea to design custom playing cards based on people he knew, and I thought it would be a great idea to do the same for Skynet. I assigned each person a face card, and when we had more people than cards, assigned others to the Joker cards and the Ace of Spades.


Back Design

Clayton had already created the original Skynet wordmark "logo", so I used that for the text on the back. Given the futuristic, sci-fi leanings of the name and font choice, I decided it would be fitting to add a band across the middle that featured a cybernetic eye, symbolizing the concept of us constantly watching. Drawing inspiration from Wheatley and the turrets in Valve's Portal games, I gave the eye spokes and a series of rings in blue.

Since I wanted the final product to be perfectly symmetrical, I made the choice to switch from my usual Paint Tool SAI to Photoshop, knowing that the latter would give me greater control over the fine details. I made extensive use of guidelines and the circle tool to ensure a perfectly aligned design.


Face Cards


I started with the face cards for the spades, beginning with Clayton himself. The initial outlines were traced off photographs, then modified to better fit the style. Each card was first given a flat color, then approximate placements of facial features, before adding basic shading. Despite the overall flat style, I wanted to give the cards a little dimension, so opted for gradient shading over cell shading. Additionally, in Clayton's card, I modified his expression to better fit a king card.



Progress and Challenges

Over time school caught up and my attentions were turned to more urgent design projects, leaving these playing cards on the back burner. As time went on, some of us have fallen out of the friend group, while new people joined in. As these changes happen, I've been reconsidering the card assignments, even as I try to find time to finish the cards currently in progress.

Additionally, these cards were some of my first attempts at learning to use Photoshop. I had actually made the majority of the King of Spades card in Paint Tool SAI, only switching over to Photoshop when I realized the need for perfect symmetry. As I've gotten more familiar with the program though, I've been more and more inclined to test my new skills on cleaning up the cards and finishing the whole batch. One day I plan to come back to these, and get them printed and distributed to the friends that inspired them.
