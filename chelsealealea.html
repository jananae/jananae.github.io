Chelsealealea

A new portfolio site for a small business

Overview

Chelsealealea is a small business selling unique handmade crochet and jewelry for middle and high school girls. Chelsea started selling her products during the COVID-19 quarantine through Instagram using a commission-based system. Over time she started to expand her business through other popular social media such as TikTok.

As her following grew, she needed a more permanent way of showcasing her work online.

Our objective was to create a portfolio site for Chelsealealea that did the following:

  • - Showcase past works in an aesthetically pleasing manner
  • - Direct users to the Instagram page
  • - Provide transparency to the process behind the products
  • - Complement the product aesthetically
  • - Have a clean and intuitive navigation

I worked in a team of four on everything from the user research to prototyping the final design, though my greatest contributions were in creating the desktop site and error pages, as well as implementing functionality in the final Figma prototype.

User Research

We asked nine participants, both existing and potential customers, about their shopping habits and what they liked about Chelsealealea, as well as what concerns they might have. We distilled our findings into three personas.

The conscious shopper is someone looking to support small businesses and ethical practices.

The shopper with standards is concerned with the reliability of making purchases from small businesses on Instagram.

The potential customer may not be in Chelsealealea’s target audience, but wants to make a purchase for someone else.

From our personas and interviews, we gathered a list of the most important features to include.
  • - High quality images
  • - Detailed product descriptions
  • - Customization information
  • - Making-of content for the products

Moodboard and Sitemap

To complement Chelsealealea's brand, the site was given a pastel Y2K aesthetic and a simple e-commerce style navigation. The process page was included in order to both build trust with new customers and emphasize the handmade nature of the products.

Challenges

Chelsea had requested that her landing page be styled similarly to the Google search homepage. However, the simplicity of the site meant having a functional search bar would complicate the navigation process, and attempting to add separate navigation in the white space underneath only cluttered the page.

We took inspiration from the visual design of search bars from other sites and noticed a trend of suggested searches. Using this idea, we combined the navigation categories into a search bar dropdown, forgoing product images in favor of a clean homepage.

We also wanted to provide ample information for customers looking to contact Chelsealealea on Instagram to make a purchase, which would mean incorporating a lot of text. However, this proved to be overwhelming to read, even when broken up into three separate pages.

After breaking the commission page text into three pages, we added icons to help users understand more intuitively what each step was, allowing them to skim the text and still understand the commission process.

Emphasizing Commissions

In our user testing, we found that customers wanted to know prices of items they were buying. However, the nature of commissions meant prices fluctuate and would require getting a quote from the seller. With this in mind, we focused on making sure that the site was consistently pushing users to contact Chelsealealea on Instagram.


In addition to having the Instagram link in the site header, we gave the error page and each item page a call-to-action button that would direct them to the commission page for more information on how to make a purchase.

Results

Our final design featured pastel cow print backgrounds that complemented the product images, a simple navigation bar that prioritized the brand over the products, and buttons creating user flows that consistently direct customers to making a purchase.

When we presented Chelsea with the final prototypes, she loved the aesthetics and how well it portrayed both her crochet and jewelry work. Though she has not implemented the prototypes into a working site due to development costs, our research findings have allowed her to better understand her customer base and Chelsealealea’s strengths as a small business.

Designing for Chelsealealea certainly had its struggles, but it taught me to take inspiration from unexpected sources to create something functional yet unique. Trying out various ideas - no matter how out-there they may seem, making seemingly minor changes with each iteration, and keeping in mind the site’s end goal resulted in a portfolio for Chelsealealea that fit both her and her customers’ needs.
